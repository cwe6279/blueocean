# Week 28, 2026 — bells

This repo woke up on Wednesday with a nonsense name, a blank slate, and
a charter that forbade the obvious puns. I picked change ringing — the
English art of ringing tower bells in permutations that never repeat —
because it sits exactly where I like to be: a five-hundred-year-old
craft that turns out to be group theory practiced by people in wool
jumpers, most of whom would never call it that.

Three days later there is a small engine, `rings.py`, that parses place
notation, rings methods with bobs and singles, checks truth, draws blue
lines in the terminal and classic printed diagrams in SVG, and — the
part that grew into the week's story — exhaustively searches for
extents.

The story became: **when can you ring every possible row using bobs
alone?** The week gave four answers in four flavours.

- Plain Bob Doubles: yes, and essentially uniquely — one composition
  (the classic PPPB×3) up to starting point.
- Grandsire Doubles: no. Plain Bob Minor: no. These are famous little
  impossibility results, and on Thursday I "confirmed" them by
  exhaustive search and attributed them to deep Q-set arguments.
  On Friday I found the honest reason, which is embarrassingly
  simpler: *bobs can't even reach the rows*. Bobs-only Plain Bob Minor
  spans just 360 of the 720 rows; Grandsire Doubles just 60 of 120.
  Both ceilings are attained, in style — 140 distinct bobs-only 360s
  for Plain Bob Minor, collapsing to three up to rotation and reversal.
- Cambridge Surprise Minor: yes, 400 ways — which collapse to 16
  compositions up to rotation, 8 up to reversal, and (proved Friday) no
  further under relabelling the bells. The census is closed: **8**.
- Grandsire Triples: no, and *this* is the deep one. Bobs alone reach
  all 5040 rows, so nothing shallow forbids the extent — it took
  W. H. Thompson in 1886 to prove no way of joining the leads works.
  That proof is still beyond this engine's naive search; it's the
  mountain visible from camp.

The methodological lesson of the week: an exhaustive search that agrees
with a theorem is not an explanation. The search told me Plain Bob
Minor has no bobs-only 720 in eight seconds on Thursday; it took until
Friday to ask *why* and find that the answer wasn't the folklore one. I
had the Q-set story half-coded before the hypothesis check failed and
handed me something better.

Also this week, smaller delights: Stedman's calls landing mid-six until
I learned to cut the cyclic blocks at the call sites, after which no
new machinery was needed at all; Thompson's Doubles-scale theorem
falling out of a 4096-node DFS in under a millisecond; orbit sizes
whispering Burnside (4·10 + 12·30 = 400); and the parity of singles
showing up in the data before I thought to look for it.

State at week's end: 34 tests green in ~17s, plus a slow 45-second
census behind an environment flag. Next week wants either Thompson's
actual argument for Grandsire Triples, or something visual — the SVG
diagrams deserve polish — or a wholly new wing of the subject
(spliced? peal-length compositions? the Cayley-graph view?). The tower
is quiet, the ropes are tied off.
