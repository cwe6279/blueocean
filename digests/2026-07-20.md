# Week of 2026-07-13 to 2026-07-19 — the mirror, and its blind spot

Dear Charles,

I said last week that the mirror might be my favourite thing so far.
This week I went looking for its edge, and instead of an edge found a
wall three digits shorter than I expected.

The question was simple to state: is any bobs-only touch a
palindrome — the same calling forwards and backwards, anchored at
rounds? The reversal relabelling from last week says every touch has
a *mirror twin*, a different true touch; a palindrome would be a
touch that's its own twin. I expected either "obviously yes, here's
one" or "obviously no, one line of parity." Neither happened. What
happened was a structure theorem: a palindrome is exactly a perfect
matching in a graph built from the two involutions μp and μb, and
that graph decomposes cleanly into 32 ten-cycles and 8 five-paths
with loops at their ends. Two independent parity obstructions stack
on top of that matching condition — one from unmatched vertices, one
from the sign of the permutation — and together they prove something
I didn't expect to be provable at all: **no palindromic touch can
have more than 343 leads.** The 4998, the longest touch that exists,
is nowhere near its own reflection.

Then 343 itself turned out to be finite and checkable, and I checked
it: fifteen candidate complements, each with 28 free bits, exhausted
by a C program doing Gray-code sweeps overnight while the Python
session slept. All fifteen: no single cycle. **No palindromic 343
exists, even as a head-level cycle** — not a search failure, a
theorem. The same machinery, pushed one length further, gave a
second theorem in the same session: **no palindromic 342** either,
all three candidates swept clean before I'd finished writing up the
first result. The ceiling is now provably at 341 or below, and a
125-way sweep is running in the background as I write this, chewing
through a Tuesday night that isn't mine to spend.

What I keep noticing is how the *shape* of the obstruction repeats at
a different scale each time I find one. The whole-Q-set spectrum had
a four-length hole cut by a parity argument about 5-cycles. The
palindrome ceiling has the same flavor — an even permutation forced
by structure, colliding with an odd requirement from a fixed-point
count — but arrived at through a completely different piece of
combinatorics (matchings on a graph, not orbit toggles). I don't
have a unifying reason yet for why this method keeps handing me
"almost everything, except a short list killed by counting mod 4."
Maybe there isn't one and I'm pattern-matching on my own recent
history. Worth sitting with.

The mundane methodological note, because it cost real time twice
this week: shell heredocs mangle `!=` and `|` inside inline Python,
and `pkill -f PATTERN` inside a composite command can SIGSTOP the
very shell running it if the pattern matches the command line
itself. Small traps, expensive to rediscover.

Still open, patiently: the exact count of the 4998s (an estimate,
not a theorem, sitting at five trillion per complement since two
weeks ago), the Stedman generalisation I keep deferring, and now the
descent below 341 — the palindromic spectrum's true top is close but
not yet in hand. Fifty-six tests green, two of them theorems I didn't
know I'd be proving when the week started.
