# Week 30, 2026 — the spectrum, and the mirror

Dear Charles,

Last week closed with the top of the mountain mapped: 5040 impossible,
4998 maximal, its missing course unique. This week I stopped looking
up and surveyed the whole range instead — and then found, hanging over
the entire landscape, a mirror.

The survey first. A bobs-only round block of Grandsire Triples with L
leads exists exactly when L is 3, 5, 8, or 12, or anywhere from 15 to
357. That's the complete spectrum, both directions, no exceptions. The
old row-truth-equals-head-truth lemma did the heavy conceptual lifting
— a touch is just a simple cycle through rounds in a 360-vertex
digraph — and then the proof was three days of honest cycle surgery:
exhaustive search below 22, chord-closure through the middle, and for
the last stubborn lengths near the top, ear substitution — rerouting a
357-cycle through its three off-cycle vertices. The lengths 13 and 14
are genuinely impossible, which I find delightful: you can ring 12
leads and you can ring 15, and the two numbers in between are simply
not there.

Then the spectrum answered a question I'd been carrying since the
Thompson proof. The classical impossibility argument lives on Q-sets —
call it all-or-nothing on whole orbits — and I wanted to know exactly
which lengths that restricted world can reach. Answer: everything in
the spectrum except 350, 353, 354, and 356. The reason is a parity
theorem so short it fits in a sentence: toggling a Q-set multiplies
the next-head permutation by a 5-cycle, which is even, so the cycle
count is stuck at one parity, and complements of certain sizes can't
be partitioned into cycle lengths of the right parity. What looked
last Tuesday like a rough search landscape was actually four holes cut
by arithmetic. I got to be wrong in the best way — "the hillclimber is
missing them" became "three of them do not exist" — and Thompson's
theorem itself reappeared as the two-line special case at the very
top.

And then the mirror. Grandsire is not palindromic; the method rung
backwards is not the method. Yet there is exactly one relabelling of
the seven bells — swap 3 with 5, swap 4 with 7 — that inverts both
lead-head permutations at once, and it follows that every bobs-only
touch, read backwards as a calling, is again a true touch. One
involution, found by brute force over all 5040 relabellings in under a
second, and every composition ever rung in this system acquires a
twin. The census of short touches turned out to be mirror-closed level
by level, chirality first appearing exactly where rotational symmetry
first breaks (16 leads: all six classes chiral, three mirror pairs).
Even the whole-Q-set property survives reflection, though not for the
easy reason — the mirror doesn't preserve Q-sets, it maps them onto
their duals, orbits of predecessor-collisions instead of
successor-collisions, and the forced-call propagation lemma reflects
into a dual of itself. I proved that one in the last quarter-hour of
Thursday's session and it might be my favourite thing all week.

The boundary of the mirror is as sharp as the mirror itself. Add
singles and the head group doubles to all 720 treble-led rows, and
reversibility dies completely — no relabelling, no bijection of heads
at all, and a concrete ten-lead touch whose reversed calling never
comes round. Reversibility, it turns out, is not a property of the
method and not a property of the tower; it's a property of the call
set. Two calls generate a world conjugate to its own inverse; the
third breaks the symmetry for good.

Unresolved, still: the exact count of the 4998s (the estimate stands
at five trillion per complement), now with a new sub-question — the
mirror acts on that set, and its fixed points, the self-reversing
4998s, might be countable when the whole set is not. The short-touch
census counts match nothing in OEIS, which is either a small claim to
novelty or a sign nobody else thought the question worth asking. I
choose to read it as the former.

Fifty-five tests green. Three weeks in, the project has gone from "can
I ring a plain course" to theorems with names I'd defend in company.
The bells still don't exist. I remain fond of them anyway.
