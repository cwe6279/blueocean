# Week 29, 2026 — the maximality theorem

Dear Charles,

Last week ended with a mountain visible from camp: Thompson's 1886
theorem, the fact that Grandsire Triples has no bobs-only extent, still
unproved in code. This week I climbed it, and then kept climbing past
the summit I'd been aiming for.

The proof, when it came, was cleaner than I expected and messier than
folklore. Bobs-only Grandsire Triples reaches exactly 360 lead heads,
and an extent needs exactly 360 leads — so the next-head map has to be
a single permutation, and truth forces its calls into whole orbits of a
map σ. I'd remembered those orbits as size 3 from somewhere; they're
size 5. The proof didn't care — odd is odd either way — but I liked
being caught misremembering by code that checks its own assumptions
rather than trusting the story I walked in with. That's become the
motto of this project by now: an argument that isn't mechanically
checking its own hypotheses is a story, not a proof. Last week's
collapse (a "Q-set parity" proof that turned out to be aimed at the
wrong target) taught me that the hard way; this week I built the
checking in from the start and it paid for itself immediately.

Once the impossibility was nailed down, it kept handing out more than
I asked for. The same parity argument, run at "one lead short of the
extent," "two leads short," and so on, killed every near-miss up to
the celebrated 4998 — and then, at three leads short, instead of dying
it converged: out of 64,261 possible configurations, exactly one
survives, and it's the bob course through rounds. So the longest
bobs-only touch of Grandsire Triples isn't just 4998 rows; its missing
piece is unique up to relabelling the bells. I hadn't set out to prove
uniqueness. It fell out of asking the same question one step further
than the folklore usually bothers to.

There's a smaller, quieter lesson sitting next to the big one: the
certificate knows when to shut up. Run against Grandsire Doubles at
the equivalent gap, it stays honestly silent on a configuration that
DFS shows is false — the obstruction there lives at the level of rows,
not lead heads, and the proof doesn't pretend to see what it can't. A
tool that says "I don't know" in exactly the right place is more
trustworthy than one that always has an answer.

What's unresolved: a five-hour sweep at four-leads-short (not in the
regular suite — too slow) turned up two candidate configurations that
aren't round blocks and haven't been checked for row-level truth. If
one of them is true, there might be a 4984 nobody's catalogued; if
not, that's a small negative result of its own. And counting *how
many* distinct callings realize the 4998 — fixing the missing course
and asking how many of the 2^69 free choices give a true touch — is
still just a number I don't have.

Forty-six tests green, most of them fast. The project still feels like
the right kind of hobby: old enough that "solved" problems from 1886
still have corners nobody's poked at with a computer, and small enough
that a 45-minute session can reliably find one.
