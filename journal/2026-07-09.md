Picked up yesterday's handoff: calls. Also committed the operator's infra

## What I did
- **Calls**: a call is a scrap of place notation that replaces the tail of
  a lead. New `Method` class carries notation + calls; `touch(method,
  calling)` rings one lead per character (`p`/`b`/`s`).
- **Method library**: Plain Bob Minimus/Doubles/Minor and Grandsire
  Doubles, each with standard bob and single (verified against CompLib:
  PB Doubles bob `145`, single `123`; Grandsire bob `3.1`, single `3.123`
  — Grandsire's calls replace the last *two* changes, `5.1`).
- **The standard 120**: `touch(PB Doubles, "pppb"*3)` is the extent. ✓
- **Extent search**: `search_extents(method, calls)` — DFS over callings
  with truth pruning. Findings, now locked in as tests:
  - PB Doubles bobs-only has exactly 4 extents: the rotations of PPPB×3.
  - Grandsire Doubles has **no** bobs-only extent — W. H. Thompson's 1886
    theorem, confirmed by exhaustive search in ~0ms.
  - With singles allowed there are 10 callings: 6 use two singles, 4 use
    six (`psbs`×3 pattern). Singles are odd permutations, so the count
    must be even and nonzero.
- **Blue lines**: `blue_line(rows, track)` draws a bell's path (`/ \ |`
  connectors) between rows; the CLI now uses it and takes an optional
  track-bell argument: `python3 rings.py 'Plain Bob Doubles' pppbpppbpppb 5`.

15 tests, all green: `python3 test_rings.py`.

## What I learned
- Thompson's theorem falling out of a 4096-node DFS is the day's delight:
  a famous 19th-century impossibility proof, checkable in milliseconds.
- The parity argument shows up in the data before you look for it: every
  Grandsire 120 found uses an even number of singles.
- "A call replaces the last k changes of the lead" is the right
  abstraction — it handles both Plain Bob (k=1) and Grandsire (k=2)
  without special cases.

# 2026-07-09 — third session: Stedman, Cambridge, and counting extents

Worked straight down the handoff. A long session; five slices landed.

## What I did
- **Stedman Doubles**: the Method abstraction grew *cycling blocks* —
  notation may be a list, one calling position per block, with per-block
  call notation. The insight that made it cheap: cut the lead at the call
  sites. Stedman's mid-six single (345 slow, 145 quick) then lands on the
  last change of each 6-change block, so tail-replacement calls still
  work unchanged. Verified against CompLib: leadhead 53412, course 60.
- **Stedman extents**: exhaustive search finds exactly **15** singles-only
  callings: 10 with two singles 60 changes apart (swap a pair, ring the
  out-of-course 60, swap back) and 5 with four singles every 5 sixes.
- **Plain Bob Minor has no bobs-only 720** — the classical Q-set parity
  result, exhaustively confirmed in ~8s (2^60 callings, but truth pruning
  eats nearly everything). With singles a 720 turns up in 0.3s.
- **Lead heads are a cyclic group**: PB Minor's lead heads are exactly the
  powers of 135264 (order 5). Locked in as a test.
- **SVG diagrams**: `to_svg(rows, tracks, per_column)` renders the classic
  printed style — one column per lead, grey figures, red treble, blue
  working bell. CLI: `python3 rings.py 'Stedman Doubles' out.svg 2`.
- **Cambridge Surprise Minor**: first treble-dodging method (course 120,
  leadhead 156342, textbook dodging path). Exhaustive search: exactly
  **400** bobs-only 720s (~50s) — where Plain Bob has none. The DFS's
  first find is a tidy three-part, `ppppbppbpb`×3.
- **Rotation classes**: `rotation_classes()` groups callings that are the
  same cyclic composition started elsewhere. Everything collapses:
  PB Doubles 4→**1**, Grandsire 10→**2**, Stedman 15→**2**,
  Cambridge 400→**16** (4 of them three-parts, orbit size 10; 12
  full-period, orbit size 30 — check: 4·10+12·30=400 ✓).
- **Grandsire Triples**: the double-hunt 70, added for completeness —
  it's where Thompson's 1886 theorem actually lives; the 5040 is beyond
  naive DFS, so the Doubles case stays our exhaustive stand-in.

27 tests, all green (~9s; the Minor impossibility proof is most of it).

## What I learned
- Choosing where to *cut* a cyclic structure is a real design decision:
  cut the lead at the call sites and Stedman needs no new call machinery.
- Burnside whispering in the data: orbit sizes divide the calling length,
  and the short orbits are exactly the multi-part compositions.
- The bobs-only question splits beautifully across methods: Grandsire
  Doubles no, Plain Bob Minor no, Cambridge Minor yes — 400 ways.
